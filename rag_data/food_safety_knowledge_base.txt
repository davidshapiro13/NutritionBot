# Food Safety & Food Poisoning: Public Health Knowledge Base
# Sources: CDC, FDA, USDA/FSIS, WHO
# Last updated: 2026-03-13

================================================================================
SECTION 1: FOOD POISONING SYMPTOMS
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/food-safety/signs-symptoms/index.html
================================================================================

The five primary symptoms of food poisoning (foodborne illness) are:
- Diarrhea
- Stomach pain or cramps
- Nausea
- Vomiting
- Fever

Symptoms depend on the type of germ (bacteria, virus, or parasite) swallowed.

IMPORTANT: The food that caused illness is often NOT the last food eaten. It
frequently takes 2–3 days to feel sick after eating contaminated food.

Symptom Onset Timeline by Pathogen:
- Staphylococcus aureus (Staph): 30 minutes to 8 hours
- Salmonella: 6 hours to 6 days
- Norovirus: 12 to 48 hours
- E. coli (STEC): 3 to 4 days (range: 2–8 days)
- Listeria: up to 2 weeks
- Clostridium perfringens: 6 to 24 hours
- Campylobacter: 2 to 5 days

Potential Long-Term Complications:
- Meningitis
- Kidney damage
- Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)
- Arthritis
- Brain or nerve damage

SECTION 2: COMMON FOODBORNE ILLNESSES
================================================================================

--- 2A. SALMONELLA (Salmonellosis) ---
Source: FDA — https://www.fda.gov/food/foodborne-pathogens/salmonella-salmonellosis
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/food-safety/data-research/facts-stats/index.html

What it is: Bacteria that cause salmonellosis. Leading cause of foodborne illness
hospitalizations and deaths in the US (~450 deaths/year).

Symptoms:
- Diarrhea, fever, and abdominal cramps (most common)
- More severe cases: high fever, aches, headaches, lethargy, rash
- Blood in urine or stool (severe cases)

Onset: 12 to 72 hours after infection.
Duration: 4 to 7 days. Most people recover without treatment.

Common Food Sources:
- Raw or undercooked meat, poultry, and eggs
- Unpasteurized dairy products
- Fruits, leafy greens, and raw sprouts
- Nut butters and spreads

At-Risk Groups: Children under 5, adults 65+, pregnant women, immunocompromised.

Treatment: Hydration. Severe cases may require antibiotics and hospitalization.

Prevention:
- Cook meat, poultry, and eggs to safe internal temperatures
- Wash surfaces; sanitize with bleach solution (1 tbsp bleach per gallon of water)
- Avoid raw sprouts (especially for at-risk groups)
- Wash hands thoroughly


--- 2B. NOROVIRUS ---
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/norovirus/about/index.html

What it is: Most common cause of foodborne illness in the US and globally.
Highly contagious virus. Sometimes incorrectly called "stomach flu."

Symptoms:
- Sudden-onset diarrhea
- Explosive vomiting
- Nausea
- Stomach pain
- Sometimes: low-grade fever, headache, body aches

Onset: 12 to 48 hours after exposure.
Duration: 1 to 3 days. Infected people can keep spreading the virus for 2+ weeks
after feeling better.

How it Spreads:
- Direct contact with an infected person
- Eating or drinking contaminated food or water
- Touching contaminated surfaces then touching mouth
- Raw shellfish (especially oysters)

Treatment: No specific medicine. Rest and stay hydrated. Severe dehydration may
require IV fluids. Antibiotics do NOT work against viruses.

IMPORTANT: Hand sanitizer is less effective against Norovirus — use soap and water.

Prevention:
- Wash hands with soap and water frequently
- Cook shellfish thoroughly (Norovirus survives up to 145°F)
- Wash all fruits and vegetables before eating
- Stay home for at least 48 hours after symptoms stop
- Disinfect contaminated surfaces
- Wash laundry in hot water


--- 2C. E. COLI (Shiga Toxin-Producing E. coli / STEC) ---
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/ecoli/signs-symptoms/index.html
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/ecoli/treatment/index.html

What it is: Most E. coli strains are harmless, but STEC (including O157:H7)
causes serious illness. Linked to leafy greens, beef, and contaminated water.

Symptoms:
- Severe stomach cramps
- Diarrhea (often bloody or watery)
- Vomiting
- Low-grade fever (less common)

Onset: 2 to 8 days (typically 3–4 days).
Duration: 5 to 7 days.

SERIOUS COMPLICATION — Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS):
HUS is life-threatening, especially in young children. Warning signs:
- Decreased or no urination
- Extreme pallor (loss of skin color)
- Unexplained bruising
- Blood in urine
- Extreme fatigue and irritability
- Altered mental state
→ HUS is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY requiring immediate hospital care.

Treatment:
- Drink extra fluids
- Do NOT use anti-diarrheal medications (Imodium) with high fever or bloody
  diarrhea — increases risk of HUS
- Most recover without antibiotics
- Call a doctor before taking any medication

Prevention:
- Wash hands after handling raw meat or using the bathroom
- Cook ground beef to 160°F internal temperature
- Avoid unpasteurized milk, juice, and cider
- Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly
- Avoid cross-contamination between raw meat and other foods


--- 2D. OTHER NOTABLE PATHOGENS ---
Source: WHO — https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/food-safety
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/food-safety/data-research/facts-stats/index.html

Listeria monocytogenes:
- Especially dangerous for pregnant women and newborns
- Can survive refrigeration temperatures
- Found in deli meats, soft cheeses, smoked seafood
- Can cause miscarriage or stillbirth in pregnant women

Campylobacter:
- One of the most common causes of diarrheal illness worldwide
- Linked mainly to raw poultry
- Onset: 2–5 days; Duration: 2–10 days

Clostridium perfringens:
- Often from large batches of food kept warm too long (cafeteria-style)
- Onset: 6–24 hours; Self-limiting, rarely fatal

Staphylococcus aureus (Staph):
- Fastest onset: 30 minutes to 8 hours
- Caused by toxins in improperly stored food
- Linked to salads, sandwiches, dairy products

Hepatitis A:
- Viral; transmitted via raw shellfish or contaminated produce
- Vaccine available

US Burden Rankings (CDC):
1. Norovirus — most illnesses overall (21 million/year)
2. Salmonella — most hospitalizations and deaths
3. Clostridium perfringens
4. Campylobacter
5. Staphylococcus aureus

SECTION 3: BASIC FOOD SAFETY GUIDELINES
================================================================================

Source: USDA FSIS — https://www.fsis.usda.gov/food-safety/safe-food-handling-and-preparation/food-safety-basics/steps-keep-food-safe
Source: WHO — https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/food-safety

--- THE FOUR CORE STEPS (USDA Framework) ---

STEP 1: CLEAN — Wash Hands and Surfaces Often
- Wash hands with soap and warm water for at least 20 seconds:
  before and after handling food, after using bathroom, after touching pets/garbage
- Wash cutting boards, dishes, utensils, countertops with hot soapy water after
  contact with raw meat, poultry, seafood, or eggs
- Rinse fresh fruits and vegetables under running water before eating/preparing
- Sanitize surfaces with bleach solution (1 tbsp unscented bleach per gallon water)

STEP 2: SEPARATE — Don't Cross-Contaminate
- Keep raw meat, poultry, seafood, and eggs separate from ready-to-eat foods
- Use separate cutting boards for raw meat vs. produce
- Keep raw meat on the bottom refrigerator shelf in sealed containers
- Never put cooked food on a plate that held raw meat without washing it first
- Bag raw meats separately when grocery shopping

STEP 3: COOK — Cook to the Right Temperature

Safe Minimum Internal Temperatures (USDA):
- Ground beef, pork, lamb, veal: 160°F (71°C)
- Beef, pork, lamb, veal steaks/roasts/chops: 145°F (63°C) with 3-min rest
- All poultry (chicken, turkey, duck): 165°F (74°C)
- Ground poultry: 165°F (74°C)
- Fish and shellfish: 145°F (63°C)
- Eggs: cook until yolk and white are firm
- Egg dishes and casseroles: 160°F (71°C)
- Leftovers: reheat to 165°F (74°C)

Always use a food thermometer — color and texture are NOT reliable alone.

STEP 4: CHILL — Refrigerate Promptly

THE DANGER ZONE: 40°F to 140°F (4°C–60°C)
Bacteria double in number every 20 minutes in this range.

Key Rules:
- Refrigerator: at or below 40°F (4°C)
- Freezer: at or below 0°F (-18°C)
- Refrigerate perishables within 2 hours (1 hour if temperature above 90°F)
- Never thaw food at room temperature
- Thaw in: refrigerator, cold water, or microwave only

Refrigerator Storage Times:
- Cooked leftovers: 3 to 4 days
- Raw ground meats: 1 to 2 days
- Raw beef, veal, pork, lamb: 3 to 5 days
- Raw poultry: 1 to 2 days
- Raw fish: 1 to 2 days
- Luncheon meats (opened): 3 to 5 days
- Hard-boiled eggs: 1 week


--- WHO's FIVE KEYS TO SAFER FOOD ---
Source: WHO — https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/food-safety

1. Keep clean — Wash hands and surfaces; protect kitchen from pests
2. Separate raw and cooked — Never mix raw meat with ready-to-eat food
3. Cook thoroughly — Especially meat, poultry, eggs, and seafood
4. Keep food at safe temperatures — Don't leave cooked food out more than 2 hours
5. Use safe water and raw materials — Clean water; fresh foods; pasteurized products


--- HIGH-RISK FOODS ---
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/food-safety/about/index.html

Handle these with extra care:
- Raw or undercooked poultry, meat, seafood, or eggs
- Raw sprouts
- Unwashed raw produce
- Cut melon left at room temperature
- Unpasteurized dairy products and juices
- Raw dough or batter (uncooked flour or raw eggs)

SECTION 4: WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL HELP AFTER FOOD POISONING
================================================================================

Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/food-safety/signs-symptoms/index.html
Source: CDC — https://www.cdc.gov/foodborne-outbreaks/what-to-do/index.html

SEEK MEDICAL CARE IMMEDIATELY IF YOU HAVE:
1. Bloody diarrhea
2. Diarrhea lasting more than 3 days
3. High fever — temperature above 102°F (38.9°C)
4. Uncontrollable vomiting (cannot keep any liquids down)
5. Signs of dehydration:
   - Very little or no urination
   - Very dry mouth and throat
   - Dizziness when standing
   - Dark-colored urine
   - Extreme thirst
   - Dry eyes / crying without tears (in children)

SPECIAL AT-RISK POPULATIONS — Contact a doctor promptly if:
- Pregnant women develop fever and flu-like symptoms (risk of Listeria)
- Young children under 5 show bloody stool, high fever, or dehydration
- Adults 65 or older experience prolonged or worsening symptoms
- Immunocompromised individuals experience any food poisoning symptoms

HUS EMERGENCY WARNING SIGNS (especially after E. coli infection):
Go to the emergency room immediately:
- Decreased or stopped urination
- Extreme paleness
- Unexplained bruising or bleeding
- Blood in urine
- Extreme fatigue or confusion

WHAT TO DO WHEN SICK:
1. Stay hydrated — sip water, clear broth, or oral rehydration solutions (Pedialyte)
2. Avoid anti-diarrheal medications (Imodium) without doctor advice, especially
   with bloody diarrhea or high fever (can worsen E. coli infection)
3. Consult a healthcare provider — stool/blood test can identify pathogen
4. Document meals — record everything eaten in the past week, restaurants visited
5. Report illness — contact local/state health department if you suspect a source

GLOBAL STATISTICS
================================================================================

US (CDC):
- 48 million people sickened by foodborne illness annually
- 128,000 hospitalizations annually
- 3,000 deaths annually

Global (WHO):
- 600 million people (1 in 10) sickened globally each year
- 420,000 deaths globally each year
- Children under 5: 40% of global foodborne disease burden (125,000 deaths/year)
- Economic cost in low/middle-income countries: $110 billion/year
